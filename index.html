Expressing Changing Quantities Using Derivatives:-:-

Derivatives are used to express how one quantity changes with respect to another. This concept is known as the rate of change and is fundamental in understanding motion, growth, and variation.

    Examples:
  • If s(t)represents displacement with respect to time, then:
  • Velocity: v(t)=ds/dt
  • Acceleration: a(t)=dv/dt
    • This shows how derivatives help model physical and engineering systems.

    First and Second Derivative Tests:-

    Derivative tests are used to determine the nature of critical points and the behavior of functions.

  • First Derivative Test: Identifies intervals where a function is increasing or decreasing
  • Second Derivative Test: Determines whether a critical point is a maximum or minimum
      • Examples:
  • For f(x)=x3−3x2+2f(x)
  • f′(x)=3x2−6xf'(x)
  • Critical points at x=0 and x=2

    • Increasing and Decreasing Functions:-

    A function is:

  • Increasing where f′(x)>0
  • Decreasing where f′(x)<0
      • This analysis helps understand trends and behavior of graphs over intervals.

    Extrema of a Function on a Closed Interval:-

    To find absolute maximum and minimum values on a closed interval [a,b][a, b][a,b], the function is evaluated at:

  • Critical points within the interval
  • Endpoints of the interval
  • The largest and smallest values among these give the extrema.
      • Examples:
    Finding maximum profit or minimum cost in engineering optimization problems.

    Concavity and Points of Inflection:-

    Concavity describes the curvature of a graph and is determined using the second derivative.

  • Concave up: f′′(x)>0
  • Concave down: f′′(x)<0
  • Point of inflection: Where concavity changes sign
      • Examples:
  • If f′′(x)=6x−6, the point of inflection occurs at x=1

    • Indeterminate Forms in Limits:-

    Some limits lead to indeterminate forms, which cannot be evaluated directly.

  • Common forms include:
  • 0/0
  • ∞/∞
      • These forms require special techniques for evaluation

    • NUMERIAL METHOD

    Bisection Method:-

    The Bisection Method finds roots by repeatedly dividing an interval where the function changes sign.
  • Simple and reliable
  • Guaranteed convergence
  • Slower compared to other methods

    • Newton–Raphson Method:-

    The Newton–Raphson Method uses tangent lines to approximate roots of equations. Faster convergence

  • Requires derivative
  • Efficient for engineering applications
      • Example:
      Solving x^3−x−1=0
